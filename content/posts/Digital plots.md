> "Unplanned hypertext sprawl is wilderness: complex and interesting, but uninviting. Interesting things await us in the thickets, but we may be reluctant to plough through the brush, subject to thorns and mosquitoes." - From [Hypertext](http://www.eastgate.com/garden/Gardens_and_Paths.html) by Mark Bernstein

Our brains are immensely complex biological computational systems. As we march headlong into a technologically expansive future with rapid access to information, we appear to be experiencing a diminishing capacity for introspection and an exponential increase in linear patterns of thought.

If not given something to do, my brain tends to have strong but ephemeral short bursts of ideas, reflections, and notions of conviction towards a goal or ideal. The unfortunate part thus far has been the *short lived* aspect of these thoughts. I have kept a personal *Flow of Consciousness* journal for a number of years in an attempt to try and let some of my thoughts and feelings just-go. For certain applications, such as dealing with human emotion, I have found this practice to be incredibly beneficial. However, I never found much utility in other aspects of my life. And while those emotions were allowed to exit my mind and body onto the pages in my journal, I never really *solved* anything. Those feelings just **were**. 

 As part of a period of reflection heading into 2025, I decided that its time to *"tend to my garden;"* the garden of my mind. By working on this little plot of the land in cyberspace, I can put down seeds of thought, nurture them, watch them grow, and give space to a little arboretum of my own design. Here, I can sit idly or actively within it, and observe them as they develop; both as a part of me, and as an emergent entity of their own. I am the keeper and the watcher of this garden forest. These thoughts are mine and I am theirs.
